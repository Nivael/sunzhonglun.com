When it was his mother's turn, she did not go to the hospital. No need to waste the money. Maybe it will get better in a few days. I know a Chinese medicine practitioner. He gave me prescriptions. Things are hard for everyone now. Whose life is easy? Haven't we all made it through? Then, on the day the son wore mourning, he dressed in white and walked past the entrance to the alley with his sister and several unfamiliar relatives, carrying the ashes. A child suddenly set off firecrackers, playing or celebrating something. He threw himself into a corner. He thought the dragonflies had come.

2.

And yet he survived. Even he could not believe that he was the one who remained. The protracted war ended, the map of the world was divided anew, and the earth traveled one step farther toward the Pleistocene. Like many survivors, he wandered without purpose past shops reopening for business and bought a handful of lottery tickets on the way. Not one won. He smiled. Soon, people stopped discussing the war and treated it as a subject under seal. Celebration of peace became most fashionable. Celebration of national victories. Celebration of the latest technology—which, just as promised before the war, would help humanity become a new humanity, replacing our hands and feet, replacing our brains and hearts.

He was past fifty and understood little of these discussions. He sat in a crowded cemetery, placed fruit and rice before his son's headstone, and wiped away dust with a rag.

Lele used to send him photographs. He did not like speaking much, and perhaps did not like writing either, so he sent only pictures. It was on the battlefield that the father learned Lele had also become a soldier. He had grown rather tall, proving that the cases of milk his father brought him as a child had not been wasted. In another photograph, Lele kissed a drone—his side's or the enemy's, there was no way to know. In any case, he made an exaggerated kissing face, thrusting his lips forward, like the series of images throughout history in which men kissed flames and gun barrels. His father threw the picture away. He possessed an insistence that, by discarding it, Lele would become some other way, as if the photograph were what wrote him.

Later came a picture of a girl. She looked especially young, as though brute force had never touched her. Perhaps Lele met her before entering the army and had finally decided to share her with Papa. Or perhaps they had only recently begun seeing each other. There was no explanation. After receiving the photograph, his father stopped himself from making sweet hallucinations, from imagining a future, because life had already taken so much from him. His comrades described a tired joy in him during those days, or a joyful tiredness. Later still, after he had returned home, a package arrived containing what his son had left: clothes, a pencil, a journal, razor, toothbrush, money, wallet, glasses case, and the letter he had once written to him. All the accidentally remaining things one person can leave.

3.

And yet he survived. Before he turned eighty, he made a long journey. He was going to the southern city to visit his sister in a nursing home. While packing, he found that he had adopted some of Mama's old habits. He arranged his necessities on a square cloth, then folded the four corners inward until it resembled a little steamed bun. First he recognized the movements. Only afterward did he remember Mama. But too much time had passed. The people once important to him, like the unimportant events he had just heard about, remained only as outlines and scattered moments. Even his own feelings when he was with Mama seemed foreign, as though he had heard them from a child and envied that child so much he imagined himself in his place.

Once his bag was packed, he chose the train from among the many new forms of transportation. Many years before, his sister had leaned against a train window in this way on her journey to the southern city. She was young then but held no particular hope for the future. She accepted. She endured. When *Homo sapiens* crossed the ice ridges of the Bering land bridge into another continent more than ten thousand years ago, was it possible they, too, expected nothing? Perhaps people do not migrate for a purpose, but are simply carried somewhere new by one foot crossing over the other, like an object without resistance moving in one direction through a vacuum at constant velocity. She migrated. Her younger brother remained. When he was nearly eighty, he came stooping to find her.

The train had once been a thrilling invention. From trains to high-speed rail, speed increased and erased the footprints in the dirt along the way, the lifting knees, the rain, the moments when leaves landed on human faces. Today, even the speed of high-speed rail provoked disgust. People wanted to close their eyes in one shot and open them in another. Perhaps what people pursue is not speed, but rupture.

He rested his head against the train window. Decades before, when crowded and silent buses still moved quickly through the city, he had sat beside his sister and watched her place her head against the glass. After each jolt knocked it away, she gently placed it back. He was old now and could not withstand the shaking, so he changed position. Then he noticed the scenery retreating outside. Many things had simply disappeared: houses covered in black tiles, pedestrians beneath the tracks, animals in fields, music played on familiar instruments. Other things had become ruins: stations that once bustled, roads, residential compounds and their streetfront shops, the spherical science museum in the city, billboards, kindergartens, rubber playgrounds, the transparent wrapper half torn from a fast-food box.

He arrived. He pushed open the door of the nursing home. It was a market for old people. They pushed little carts and crossed one another slowly, speaking loudly, as though furious bargaining had been slowed many times over. His sister sat in a wheelchair at the far window with her back to him. He remembered an afternoon many years before. He sat on her bicycle with his small arms around her waist, looking at the back of her head. She opened her hands and released the handlebars. They were about to go downhill.

**Break.**

But he died long ago, his sister said. She sank her head into the wheelchair. She was very frightened, as though trying to force herself to slide out of it. So you're not my brother. You're lying, she said.

Sister, it's me, he said.

Ah! his sister cried, but her voice was no louder than the conversation of the other residents, so no one but him heard.

Are you the police, or the boss? Did I do something wrong? I'm sorry, she said.

I'm your brother. You recognize me, he said.

My brother was born in 1990. He's seven this year. But you're an old man, she said.

He laughed. We're both old now.

No. My brother died long ago, she said.

He had a strong life, he said.

A little moisture seeped from the dense wrinkles around her eyes. Do you know, maybe I killed him. It was all because of me...

He came close. He survived, Sister. He lived until now, all because his sister looked after him.

She furrowed her brow again. It's strange when you say that. You're an old man. I can see that, can't I?

This time, he said nothing. He patted her shoulder. Her eyes moved in every direction.

Where are we? she asked. I can't bear this place. Can we go ride the bicycle?

By the time he pushed his sister's wheelchair onto the balcony, she was asleep. He drew the blanket over her and stood at the railing. The daylight was bright. He had to shade his eyes loosely with one hand.

Where are we. Did we ever arrive anywhere. What is hallucination and what is real. What is love? Everything born before our eyes—is it made by human beings, or a free combination of shape, color, and smell? We do not have much time left. Then let us open our eyes wide, turn back one more time, and look once more at what we clung to, what we avoided, what happened.
